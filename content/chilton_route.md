# Mary Chilton Route — Time Warp: Plymouth or Jamestown

> Plymouth-side child-survivor lens. Mary Chilton (1607–1679), Mayflower passenger, orphaned at 13, Plymouth matriarch.
> Voice: strict first-person present tense per §1.5 HARD RULE. Voice MATURES across the route — starts at 13 (fearful, observant, devout-but-domestic), ends at 72 (matriarch, weighty, Boston widow with a will to write).
> Tone: Pilgrim register, gentler than Bradford's chronicler weight, more inward. Female + child + orphan + survivor lens.
> Music: shared Bradford-cousin track (Vince-Guaraldi-cousin jazz piano trio, autumn-melancholy) for now. Per-slide ambient SFX layered: cabin creak / waves / shore / hearth / forest / Boston street.
> Anchor SFX used: organ chord (Compact reveal slide 6, distant — she signs it as a child), wind-howl (winter death-toll, slide 9), dinner-bell (Thanksgiving, slide 14).

## Slide 6 — Plymouth Rock (and the Compact, Heard Through Boards)

**Image brief:** Mary at the bow of the shallop as it grounds on a flat shore-stone. Her shoes still on the boat, one foot lifting toward the rock. Other passengers behind her, a man with a musket already on the shore scanning the trees. Bare winter forest in the middle distance, a dusting of snow. Period-art aesthetic, sober, no triumph in the composition — this is arrival, not victory.
**Audio:** Piano holds a single warm phrase. Surf on stone, very close. A single distant low organ chord on slide load — the Mayflower Compact anchor SFX, but quieter than Bradford's version, because Mary heard it through ship's planking, not as a signer. Wind on the shore.
**Timeline bar:** New tick illuminates and solidifies: `Nov 1620 — Mayflower Compact signed.` Then `Dec 1620 — Plymouth founded.`
**Slide text overlay:** Bone-white card.

**Body text:**
> The shallop grinds against a flat shore-stone. I step off — early, if not first. The boys will tell that story different ways for the rest of my life. The stone under my shoe is the coldest thing I have ever stood on.
> Before they let us off, forty-one of the men signed a paper in the cabin. They called it a Compact. I am thirteen. I do not yet understand what they have done.

**KEY TERMS:**
- **Mayflower Compact** — Signed Nov 11, 1620 by 41 men. They agreed to obey laws they would together make — the first written self-government on this coast.
- **Saints & Strangers** — *Saints* = Separatists (Pilgrims). *Strangers* = everyone else (tradesmen, soldiers like Standish). The Compact bound BOTH — the seed of American self-government.

## Slide 9b — We Were Not Alone

**Image brief:** Interior of a single Plymouth common-house, lit by hearth and tallow candles. Six or seven pallets on the floor with sick bodies under wool blankets — adults and children both. Two figures move between them: Elder Brewster carrying a wooden bowl of broth, and a healthy boy Mary's age helping him steady a head to drink. Mary herself kneels at one pallet wringing a cloth into a basin. A Bible lies open on a stool, a candle bent over it. Standish stands by the door in a buff coat, hat off, head bowed in prayer over a man's body wrapped in a blanket. The mood is not despair — it is *labor*. Period-engraving aesthetic, soft warm light, no triumph and no horror, just the work of survival held together by faith.
**Audio:** Piano holds a low warm hymn-figure underneath. Hearth-fire crackle. A man coughs once. A woman murmurs psalmody — *Psalm 23* almost-whispered, just a thread of melody, no words clear. Wind outside, but quieter — we are *inside* the suffering now, not exposed to it.
**Timeline bar:** Same tick — `Winter 1620–21` — held bright. Subtitle on the bar: *The seven who could still stand fed the ninety-five who could not.*
**Slide text overlay:** Bone-white card.

**Body text:**
> I am not alone. That is the part the books leave out.
> By February, only six or seven of us can still stand. Six or seven, to care for all the rest.
> Elder Brewster carries broth. Captain Standish — a Stranger, his wife already buried — washes the sick. They tend people who are not their family. I am thirteen, and I can still stand. So I carry water too.
> When four die in one night and the ground is frozen too hard to bury them, we sing. Psalm 23. Low, so the woods won't count our voices.
> We suffer together. We praise God together. Half of us go into the ground. The other half goes on — bound to each other by a winter no king ordered and no king ended.

**KEY TERMS:**
- **General Sickness** — The Plymouth name for the illness that killed half the colony in winter 1620–21 (scurvy, pneumonia, exposure).
- **Communal Care** — The few healthy Pilgrims nursed the sick — Saints AND Strangers — even those not their own family. The Compact was being *lived*, not just signed.
- **Psalm-Singing** — Pilgrims sang Psalm 23 over the sick and dying. Praising God in suffering, not in spite of it.

> I tell the widow I will speak for the women of the colony in council. I am fifteen and stubborn, and I have not yet learned what the colony does to women who stand up in meetings.
> The first time I rise, the men do not shout — they simply do not call on me. The second time, the elders ask my widow if the orphan girl is well. The third time, I am told the rod awaits me.

**Body text:**
> The historical Mary Chilton was thirteen years old on the Mayflower. Both her parents died the first winter. She was fostered into another Pilgrim household. Family tradition — recorded by descendants in the 18th and 19th centuries — names her as the first European woman to step ashore at Plymouth Rock; we cannot prove or disprove it, but the story is old enough to be worth telling.
> She married John Winslow, the younger brother of Edward Winslow, in 1624 at age seventeen. They had ten children; eight survived. The family moved to Boston in the 1650s. John died in 1670. > Her will, preserved in Suffolk County probate records, includes a bequest of an enslaved girl named Jane. This is not invented. Plymouth and Massachusetts Bay had small-scale household slavery throughout the 17th and 18th centuries — fewer enslaved Africans than Virginia by orders of magnitude, but the institution was real, and it was perpetuated, generation by generation, by clauses like the one in Mary Chilton's will.
> John Rolfe's route, on the Jamestown side of this game, covers 1619 — the year the first enslaved Africans were brought to Virginia. Mary Chilton's route covers the parallel beat for Plymouth, just smaller and quieter and sixty years later. Both are real. Both are American. Both are how the country learned the habits it would have to fight, two centuries later, to break.
> Mary Chilton was an orphan, a survivor, a mother, a widow, a matriarch — and a slaveholder, in the last and quietest of her roles. She was all of these things at once. So was the colony she came over on.
