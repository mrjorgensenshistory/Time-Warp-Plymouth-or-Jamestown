> Jamestown side. Soldier-of-fortune lead. ~27 story slides + 10 gameovers. 11 decision points.
> Voice: strict first-person present-tense, "I" voice. Smith — blunt, plainspoken, occasionally funny, never gallant. Per §1.5 Voice & Immersion HARD RULE.

> Decision density target: ~1 decision per 2–3 story slides, matching Time-Warp-IV-Civil-War complexity. Many decisions are SOFT (both branches reconverge with different flavor). Several branch to NEW gameovers that teach the cost of the wrong call.

## Slide 27 — Epilogue: What Really Happened

**Image brief:** A clean parchment card. No portrait. Just the historical facts laid out as a list, in the same ship's-log register as Roanoke. The Disney *Pocahontas* visual mood drops away here — this is the historian's card.
**Audio:** Music bed at quarter volume. No SFX.
**Timeline bar:** Hidden — this card is outside the chronicle.
**Slide text overlay:** Parchment scroll, `IM Fell English` for the heading, Share Tech for the body.

**Body text:**
> **What the records actually say:**
> John Smith was president of Jamestown from September 1608 to September 1609. He instituted the work-or-eat rule. Under his leadership the colony survived two winters that killed most of its members.
> Smith wrote the famous Pocahontas-rescue story himself, years after the fact, in a book he sold for money. Historians today are split on whether the rescue happened, whether it was a ritual adoption Smith misread, or whether he embellished it. We do not know which. We may never know.
> Smith was wounded by a gunpowder accident in October 1609 and forced to return to England. He never saw Virginia again. The winter that followed his departure — the **Starving Time** — killed roughly 80% of the Jamestown colonists. Some ate the dead. The records are explicit.
> Pocahontas was taken hostage by the English in 1613, baptized, married John Rolfe, traveled to England, and died at twenty-one in 1617 before she could sail home.
> Smith's maps shaped English colonial efforts for the next forty years. He named the region "New England" — the same shore the Pilgrims would land on three years before he died.
